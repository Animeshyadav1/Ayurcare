Hypertension Overview

Key Facts

  • Approximately 1.28 billion adults aged 30–79 years have hypertension.
  • 46% of adults with hypertension are unaware of their condition.
  • 42% of individuals with hypertension are diagnosed and treated.
  • 21% of people with hypertension have it under control.
  • Hypertension is a major cause of premature death worldwide.
  • The global target is to reduce hypertension prevalence by 33% by 2030.

Overview

Hypertension is a condition where blood pressure is too high (≥140/90 mmHg). It is common and can be serious if untreated, as it may cause heart disease, stroke, and kidney damage.

People with hypertension often don’t feel symptoms, so regular blood pressure checks are important.

Risk Factors

Modifiable Risk Factors

  • Unhealthy diet (high salt, saturated fats)
  • Physical inactivity
  • Tobacco use
  • Excessive alcohol consumption
  • Overweight or obesity

Non-Modifiable Risk Factors

  • Age (over 65 years)
  • Family history of hypertension
  • Co-existing diseases (e.g., diabetes or kidney disease)

Symptoms

Most people with hypertension don’t feel symptoms. However, very high blood pressure (≥180/120 mmHg) can cause:

  • Severe headaches
  • Chest pain
  • Dizziness
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Blurred vision
  • Anxiety
  • Confusion
  • Buzzing in the ears
  • Abnormal heart rhythm

Treatment

Lifestyle changes such as eating a healthy diet, losing weight, and being more physically active are essential for lowering high blood pressure. Some may also need medication.

Common Medications

  • ACE inhibitors (e.g., enalapril)
  • ARBs (e.g., losartan)
  • Calcium channel blockers (e.g., amlodipine)
  • Diuretics (e.g., hydrochlorothiazide)

Prevention

Adopting healthy lifestyle changes is key to preventing and managing hypertension:

  • Eat more vegetables and fruits
  • Be physically active
  • Limit alcohol and avoid tobacco
  • Reduce salt intake
  • Monitor blood pressure regularly

Complications of Uncontrolled Hypertension

If left untreated, hypertension can lead to severe complications:

  • Heart attack
  • Heart failure
  • Stroke
  • Kidney damage

Hypertension in Low- and Middle-Income Countries

The prevalence of hypertension is increasing in low- and middle-income countries, mainly due to a rise in risk factors. The WHO African Region has the highest hypertension prevalence (27%).
